Mental Capacity Act

5 Statutory Principles

The Mental Capacity Act 2005 (MCA) provides a legal framework for making decisions on behalf of people who for any reason lack the mental capacity to make their own decisions.

The 5 Statutory Principles

Principle 1 Presumption of Capacity

A person must be assumed to have capacity unless it is established that they lack capacity. You cannot assume a person lacks capacity just because of their age, disability, appearance or condition.

Principle 2 Support to Make Decisions

A person is not to be treated as unable to make a decision unless all practicable steps to help them to do so have been taken without success. This might involve using different communication methods or choosing a specific time of day.

Principle 3 Unwise Decisions

A person is not to be treated as unable to make a decision merely because they make an unwise decision. People have the right to make choices that others might think are strange or risky, provided they have the capacity to understand the risks.

Principle 4 Best Interests

An act done, or decision made, under this Act for or on behalf of a person who lacks capacity must be done, or made, in their best interests. This involves consulting with family, friends, and considering the person's past wishes or values.

Principle 5 Least Restrictive Option

Before the act is done, or the decision is made, regard must be had to whether the purpose for which it is needed can be as effectively achieved in a way that is less restrictive of the person's rights and freedom of action.

The Two-Stage Functional Test

To establish if a person lacks capacity, you must apply the functional test:

  1. Does the person have an impairment of, or a disturbance in the functioning of, the mind or brain?
  2. Does that impairment or disturbance mean the person is unable to make the specific decision at the time it needs to be made?
